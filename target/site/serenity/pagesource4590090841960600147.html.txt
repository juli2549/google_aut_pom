<p><b>W</b> or <b>w</b> is the 23rd <a href="/wiki/Letter_(alphabet)" title="Letter (alphabet)">letter</a> of the <a href="/wiki/English_alphabet" title="English alphabet">modern English</a> and <a href="/wiki/ISO_basic_Latin_alphabet" title="ISO basic Latin alphabet">ISO basic Latin alphabets</a>. It usually represents a <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonant</a>, but in some languages (and occasionally in English), it represents a <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowel</a>. Its name in English is <a href="/wiki/English_alphabet#Letter_names" title="English alphabet"><i>double-u</i></a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[note 1]</a></sup> plural <i>double-ues</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WondersoftheInvisibleWorld-1693.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/WondersoftheInvisibleWorld-1693.jpg/220px-WondersoftheInvisibleWorld-1693.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="324" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/WondersoftheInvisibleWorld-1693.jpg/330px-WondersoftheInvisibleWorld-1693.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/26/WondersoftheInvisibleWorld-1693.jpg 2x" data-file-width="397" data-file-height="584"></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WondersoftheInvisibleWorld-1693.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1693 book printing that uses the "double u" alongside the modern letter</div></div></div>
<p>The sounds <span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labial%E2%80%93velar_approximant" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced labialvelar approximant">w</a>/</span> (spelled V) and <span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_stop" title="Voiced bilabial stop">b</a>/</span> (spelled B) of <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">Classical Latin</a> developed into a <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiced bilabial fricative">bilabial fricative</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">//</span> between <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowels</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Early Medieval Latin</a>. Therefore, V no longer adequately represented the <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labial-velar_approximant" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced labial-velar approximant">labial-velar approximant</a> <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span> of <a href="/wiki/Common_Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Germanic">Germanic phonology</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">Germanic</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span> phoneme was therefore written as VV or uu (<a href="/wiki/U" title="U">u</a> and <a href="/wiki/V" title="V">v</a> becoming distinct only by the <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern period">Early Modern period</a>) by the earliest writers of <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a>, in the 7th or 8th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gothic_alphabet" title="Gothic alphabet">Gothic</a> (not <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin-based</a>), by contrast, had simply used a letter based on the Greek <a href="/wiki/Upsilon" title="Upsilon"></a> for the same sound in the 4th century. The digraph VV/uu was also used in <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> to represent Germanic names, including Gothic ones like <a href="/wiki/Wamba,_Visigothic_king" class="mw-redirect" title="Wamba, Visigothic king">Wamba</a>.
</p><p>It is from this uu <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">digraph</a> that the modern name "double U" derives. The digraph was commonly used in the spelling of Old High German, but only in the earliest texts in Old English, where the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span> sound soon came to be represented by the <a href="/wiki/Rune" class="mw-redirect" title="Rune">runic</a>  <i><a href="/wiki/Wynn" title="Wynn">wynn</a></i>. In early <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>, following the 11th-century <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest of England">Norman Conquest</a>, uu gained popularity again and by 1300 it had taken wynn's place in common use.
</p><p>Scribal realization of the digraph could look like a pair of Vs whose branches crossed in the middle. An obsolete, cursive form found in the nineteenth century in both English and German was in the form of an n whose rightmost branch curved around as in a cursive v.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The shift from the digraph VV to the distinct ligature W is thus gradual, and is only apparent in <a href="/wiki/Abecedarium" title="Abecedarium">abecedaria</a>, explicit listings of all individual letters. It was probably considered a separate letter by the 14th century in both <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_Low_German" title="Middle Low German">Middle German</a> orthography, although it remained an outsider, not really considered part of the Latin alphabet proper, as expressed by <a href="/wiki/Valentin_Ickelshamer" title="Valentin Ickelshamer">Valentin Ickelshamer</a> in the 16th century, who complained that:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Poor <i>w</i> is so infamous and unknown that many barely know either its name or its shape, not those who aspire to being Latinists, as they have no need of it, nor do the Germans, not even the schoolmasters, know what to do with it or how to call it; some call it <i>we</i>, [... others] call it <i>uu</i>, [...] the Swabians call it <i>auwawau</i><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Middle_High_German" title="Middle High German">Middle High German</a> (and possibly already in late Old High German), the West Germanic phoneme <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span> became realized as <span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">v</a>]</span>; this is why, today, the German w represents that sound. There is no phonological distinction between <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[w]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[v]</span> in contemporary German.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_writing_systems">Use in writing systems</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English">English</span></h3>
<p>English uses w to represent <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span>. There are also a number of words beginning with a written w that is <a href="/wiki/Silent_letter" title="Silent letter">silent</a> in most dialects before a (pronounced) r, remaining from usage in <a href="/wiki/Old_English_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English language">Old English</a> in which the w was pronounced: <i>wreak, wrap, wreck, wrench, wroth, wrinkle</i>, etc. Certain dialects of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_English" title="Scottish English">Scottish English</a> still distinguish this digraph. In the Welsh loanword <a href="/wiki/Cwm_(landform)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cwm (landform)">cwm</a> it retains the Welsh pronunciation, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">//</span>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_languages">Other languages</span></h3>
<p>In Europe, there are only a few languages with w in native words, all in a central-western European zone between Cornwall and Poland: English, German, <a href="/wiki/Low_German_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Low German language">Low German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frisian_languages" title="Frisian languages">Frisian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">Walloon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kashubian_language" title="Kashubian language">Kashubian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sorbian_languages" title="Sorbian languages">Sorbian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wymysorys_language" title="Wymysorys language">Wymysorys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resian_dialect" title="Resian dialect">Resian</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">Scandinavian dialects</a>. German, Polish, Wymysorys and Kashubian use it for the <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">voiced labiodental fricative</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v/</span> (with Polish, related Kashubian and Wymysorys using <a href="/wiki/%C5%81" title=""></a> for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span>), and Dutch uses it for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">//</span>. Unlike its use in other languages, the letter is used in <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> to represent the vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/u/</span> as well as the related approximant consonant <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span>.
</p><p>The following languages historically used w for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v/</span> in native words, but later replaced it by v: <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_language" title="Lithuanian language">Lithuanian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%81acinka" class="mw-redirect" title="acinka">Belarusian acinka</a>. In Swedish and Finnish, traces of this old usage may still be found in proper names.
</p><p>Modern <a href="/wiki/German_dialects" title="German dialects">German dialects</a> generally have only <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[v]</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[]</span> for West Germanic <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span>, but <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[w]</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[]</span> is still heard allophonically for w, especially in the clusters schw, zw, and qu. Some Bavarian dialects preserve a "light" initial <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[w]</span>, such as in <i>wuoz</i> (Standard German <i>wei</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[vas]</span> '[I] know'). The Classical Latin <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[]</span> is heard in the Southern German greeting <i>Servus</i> ('hello' or 'goodbye').
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, w became a <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_approximant" title="Labiodental approximant">labiodental approximant</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">//</span> (with the exception of words with -eeuw, which have <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/e/</span>, or other diphthongs containing -uw). In many Dutch-speaking areas, such as <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">//</span> pronunciation is used at all times.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Finnish_alphabet" class="mw-redirect" title="Finnish alphabet">Finnish</a>, w is seen as a variant of v and not a separate letter. It is, however, recognized and maintained in the spelling of some old names, reflecting an earlier German spelling standard, and in some modern loan words. In all cases, it is pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">//</span>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Danish_alphabet" class="mw-redirect" title="Danish alphabet">Danish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_alphabet" class="mw-redirect" title="Norwegian alphabet">Norwegian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swedish_alphabet" title="Swedish alphabet">Swedish</a>, w is named double-v and not double-u. In these languages, the letter only exists in old names, loanwords and foreign words. (Foreign words are distinguished from loanwords by having a significantly lower level of integration in the language.) It is usually pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v/</span>, but in some words of English origin it may be pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> The letter was officially introduced in the Danish and Swedish alphabets as late as 1980 and 2006, respectively, despite having been in use for much longer. It had been recognized since the conception of modern Norwegian, with the earliest official orthography rules of 1907.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> W was earlier seen as a variant of v, and w as a letter (double-v) is still commonly replaced by v in speech (e.g. WC being pronounced as VC, www as VVV, WHO as VHO, etc.) The two letters were sorted as equals before w was officially recognized, and that practice is still recommended when sorting names in Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> In modern slang, some native speakers may pronounce w more closely to the origin of the loanword than the official <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v/</span> pronunciation.
</p><p>Multiple dialects of Swedish and Danish use the sound however. In Denmark notably in <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Jutland</a>, where the northern half use it extensively in <a href="/wiki/Jutlandic_dialect" title="Jutlandic dialect">traditional dialect</a>, and multiple places in Sweden. It is used in southern Swedish, for example in Halland where the words "wesp" (wisp) and "wann" (water) are traditionally used.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> In northern and western Sweden there are also dialects with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span>. <a href="/wiki/Elfdalian" title="Elfdalian">Elfdalian</a> is a good example, which is one of many dialects where the <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse_orthography" title="Old Norse orthography">Old Norse difference between v (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span>) and f (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v/</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/f/</span>)</a> is preserved. Thus "warg" from Old Norse "vargr", but "v" from Old Norse "hafa".
</p><p>In the alphabets of most modern Romance languages (excepting far northern French and <a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">Walloon</a>), w is used mostly in foreign names and words recently borrowed (<i>le week-end</i>, <i>il watt</i>, <i>el kiwi</i>). The digraph ou is used for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span> in native French words; oi is <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/wa/</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span>. In Spanish, Italian, and Portuguese, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[w]</span> is a non-syllabic variant of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/u/</span>, spelled u.
</p><p>The Japanese language uses "W", pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/daburu/</span>, as an ideogram meaning "double".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> It is also a short form of an <a href="/wiki/LOL#Commonly_used_equivalents_in_other_languages" title="LOL">Internet slang term</a> for "www", used to denote laughter, which is derived from the word <i>warau</i> (, meaning "to laugh"). Variations of this slang include <i>kusa</i> (, meaning "grass"), which have originated from how "www" looks.
</p><p>In Italian, while the letter w is not considered part of the standard <a href="/wiki/Italian_alphabet" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian alphabet">Italian alphabet</a>, the character is often used in place of <i>Viva</i> (hooray for...), generally in the form in which the branches of the Vs cross in the middle, at least in <a href="/wiki/Handwriting" title="Handwriting">handwriting</a> (in fact it could be considered a <a href="/wiki/Monogram" title="Monogram">monogram</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> The same symbol written upside down indicates <i>abbasso</i> (down with...).
</p><p>in the <a href="/wiki/Kokborok" title="Kokborok">Kokborok language</a>, w represents the <a href="/wiki/Open-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Open-mid back rounded vowel">open-mid back rounded vowel</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">//</span>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>, w is called <i><i lang="vi" title="Vietnamese language text">v p</i></i>, from the French <i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">double v</i></i>. It is not included in the standard <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_alphabet" title="Vietnamese alphabet">Vietnamese alphabet</a>, but it is often used as a substitute for <i>qu-</i> in <a href="/wiki/Literary_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Literary dialect">literary dialect</a> and very informal writing.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> It's also commonly used for abbreviating <i></i> in formal documents, for example <i>Trung ng</i> is abbreviated as TW<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> even in official documents and document ID number<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>"W" is the 24th letter in the <a href="/wiki/Filipino_alphabet" title="Filipino alphabet">Modern Filipino Alphabet</a> and has its English name. However, in the old Filipino alphabet, <a href="/wiki/Abakada" class="mw-redirect" title="Abakada">Abakada</a>, it was the 19th letter and had the name "wah".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup"><span title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">is that 'h' a glottal stop?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Washo_language" title="Washo language">Washo</a>, lower-case w represents a typical <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span> sound, while upper-case W represents a <a href="/wiki/Voicelessness" title="Voicelessness">voiceless</a> w sound, like the difference between English <i>weather</i> and <i>whether</i> for those who maintain the distinction.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_systems">Other systems</span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">w</span> is used for the <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labial-velar_approximant" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced labial-velar approximant">voiced labial-velar approximant</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span></h2>
<p>W is the symbol for the chemical element <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, after its German (and alternative English) name, <i>Wolfram</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> It is also the SI symbol for the <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a>, the standard unit of power.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span></h2>
<p>Double-u, whose name reflects stages in the letter's evolution when it was considered two of the same letter, a double U, is the only modern English letter whose name has more than one syllable.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[note 2]</a></sup>  It is also the only English letter whose name is not pronounced with any of the sounds that the letter typically makes in words, with the exception of <a href="/wiki/H" title="H">H</a> for some speakers.
</p><p>Some speakers shorten the name "double u" into "dub-u" or just "dub"; for example, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Wisconsin">University of Wisconsin</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wyoming" title="University of Wyoming">University of Wyoming</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Waterloo" title="University of Waterloo">University of Waterloo</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Western_Cape" title="University of the Western Cape">University of the Western Cape</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Western_Australia" title="University of Western Australia">University of Western Australia</a> are all known colloquially as "U Dub", and the automobile company <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a>, abbreviated "VW", is sometimes pronounced "V-Dub".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> The fact that many website URLs require a "<a href="/wiki/Www." class="mw-redirect" title="Www.">www.</a>" prefix has been influential in promoting these shortened pronunciations, as many speakers find the phrase "double-u double-u double-u" inconveniently long.
</p><p>In other <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a>, including German (but not Dutch, in which it is pronounced w), its name is similar to that of English <a href="/wiki/V" title="V">V</a>. In many languages, its name literally means "double v": Portuguese <i>duplo v</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[note 3]</a></sup> Spanish <i>doble ve</i> (though it can be spelled <i>uve doble</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[note 4]</a></sup> French <i>double v</i>, <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> <i>tvfalt vaff</i>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a> <i>dvojit v</i>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> <i>kaksois-vee</i>, etc.
</p><p>Former U.S. president <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> was given the nickname "Dubya" after the colloquial pronunciation of his middle initial in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, where he spent much of his childhood.
</p><p><span id="Codes_for_computing"></span>
